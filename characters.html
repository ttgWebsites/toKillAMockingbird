Characters

  • Jean Louise Finch (Scout)
  • Scout is a young and precocious girl, she would also be considered a tomboy.
  • At the beginning of the novel she seems to show a strong faith in humanity, however as she witnesses the hatred held by those in her town, her faith is put to the test.
  • She evolves into a much more mature character as the novel progresses. She also manages to overcome her issues with humanity and understand the world better.
  • Jeremy Atticus Finch ( Jem)
  • Jem is Scout's brother and closest friend throughout the novel.
  • He is nearly four years older than her, and the age difference seems to cause them to grow apart in interests as the novel progresses.
  • Much like Scout, he faces the same
  • Atticus Finch
  • Atticus is the father of both Jem and Scout, and raises them alone as his wife has died.
  • He works as a lawyer in Maycomb, and is a man of high integrity and morals.
  • He is very committed in racial justice, one of the main premises of the novel.
  • Arthur Radley (Boo)
  • Boo is described as being an outcast as he, for the most part, remains in his home not interacting with anyone.
  • Boo, despite his creepy nature, is one of the few morally sound characters in the novel.
  • He saves Scout and Jem during the novel when they are in harms way.
  • Nathan Radley
  • Nathan is Boo's older brother, he is much less kind than Boo.
  • He proves this when he covers up a knothole where Boo had been leaving gifts for the other children.
  • Heck Tate
  • Heck works in Maycomb as a sheriff and testifies in the trial of Tom Robinson.
  • He is a moral sheriff and tries to uphold the law to the best of his ability.
  • Mr. Raymond
  • Raymond is a wealthy white man, who surprisingly lives with his black mistress and their children.
  • He passes himself off as the town drunk, so as to avoid scrutiny for his relationship.
  • He is a wise and insightful man who recognizes the vile nature of prejudice.
  • Mr. Walter Cunningham
  • Cunningham is a poor man who leads a group seeking to lynch Tom, however after seeing Scout's compassion, his morals change as well.
  • Walter Cunningham
  • Walter is Mr. Cunningham's son, he attends school with Scout and Jem, and is a classmate of Scout.
  • Charles Baker Harris (Dill)
  • Dill is a neighbor of the Finches and becomes good friends with Scout and Jem.
  • He is a very adventurous young boy and harbors a fascination for the mysterious man, Boo Radley.
  • Bob Ewell
  • He is one of the novel's antagonists and is a vile, often drunk man.
  • The novel stems around a problem started by Ewell, he claims Tom Robinson, a black man, raped his daughter, however this accusation is entirely false.
  • Mayella Ewell
  • Mayella is Bob's daughter, she is abused by her father, and livers a pitiful life.
  • She claims that Tom Robinson raped her, and despite her sad lifestyle, her actions are still unforgivable.
  • Miss Atkinson
  • Miss Atkinson is a neighbor of the Finches and is very close to the family, particularly the children.
  • She is a kind-hearted woman, and has a strong moral foundation.
  • Aunt Alexandra
  • Alexandra is a strong-willed southern woman, who stands by her southern morals, even if it clashes with the rest of the Finch family.
  • She is Atticus' sister and very close to the family, despite some issues with Scout over her beliefs.
  • Calpurnia
  • Calpurnia is the Finches' cook.
  • She plays an important role, as she is black, and thus connects the young Finch children to the black community.
  • Tom Robinson
  • Tom is a kind, innocent, black farm-hand whose character is ravaged by the events in the novel.
  • He is accused of rape by Mayella Ewell, thus putting him in harms way and changing him forever.
  • Link Deas
  • Link employs Tom, in doing so he shows that he holds no prejudices against blacks, an admirable trait for a southerner in this era.
  • Mrs. Dubose
  • She is another neighbor of the Finches and is stuck in her southern ways.
  • Mrs. Dubose is a very racist woman who spends the novel battling an addiction to morphine.
