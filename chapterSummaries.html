Chapter Summaries

  • Chapter 1
  • The novel begins with the narrator and protagonist of the story, Jean Louise Finch (better known as scout) explaining the events that lead to her brother breaking his arm a few years earlier.
  • Scout begins the story with the origins of her family and explaining how they came to settle in the south.
  • The first member of the Finch family to arrive in America was a man named Simon, who worked as a fur-trader.
  • Simon sought religious freedom from his homeland of England and thus found that near the Alabama River in America.
  • For many years, the Finches' primary goal was tending the farm. It wasn't until Atticus and his brother Jack were struck out on their own did they make a name for themselves off the farm.
  • Atticus went to work in a nearby town, Maycomb, as a lawyer, while Jack set out to Boston to attend medical school.
  • Their sister, Alexandra, meanwhile chose to stay on the farm.
  • Atticus built himself a successful practice in Maycomb where he raises his two children, Scout and Jem, in a home on the main street.
  • Atticus is a widower, and therefore has a hired black cook, Calpurnia, to help him around the house.
  • The story picks up in 1933 at the beginning of the summer.
  • Scout, the protagonist, is nearly six years old, and her brother, Jem, is ten.
  • When the summer starts, the two are pleased to learn that a new neighbor has moved in next door.
  • A young boy by the name of Charles Baker Harris, however he comes to be known as Dill.
  • Dill lives with his aunt, Miss Haverford, for the summer, and he becomes very close with the Finch children.
  • The three spend all summer playing various games and getting into shenanigans.
  • At one point in the summer, Dill convinces the other children to help him lure a neighbor boy out of his house.
  • The boy in question is Arthur Radley, or Boo, who lives in a home down the street, however he is quite a loner.
  • He is much older than the three children, and is kept up in his house after he ran into trouble with the law many years ago.
  • His father essentially imprisoned him in the house; eventually his father left, and Boo's brother Nathan came to live with him.
  • Dill tries his best to convince Scout and Jem to approach Boo, however at first he is only able to convince Jem to run up and touch the house.
  • No one seems to notice, however it appears as if perhaps a shutter in a window moves slightly when this occurs.
  • Chapter 2
  • The summer ends and Dill moves away, in the meantime, Scout is gearing up for her first year of school.
  • Scout is very excited at first, however after meeting her teacher, Miss Fisher, she is disappointed.
  • Miss Caroline Fisher is very strict and harsh towards the students causing Scout to seek out Jem during a break.
  • The rest of the day does not go any better, as a misunderstanding involving another student cause Scout to be struck by a ruler.
  • A poor student, Walter Cunningham, has not brought a lunch, and Miss Caroline offers to pay for his lunch if he pays her back.
  • Scout realizes that this will not be possible as the Cunningham family is very poor, and tries to explain that, however Miss Caroline refuses to listen, and thus strikes Scout.
  • Chapter 3
  • Following the encounter when the students' break for lunch, Scout is furious with Walter, and pushes him in the dirt, however Jem sees this and breaks it up.
  • Jem apologizes and invites Walter over for lunch that day.
  • At the Finch home Walter has a long conversation with Atticus regarding farming.
  • Calpurnia makes them all lunch, and to Scout's surprise, Walter puts molasses all over his food.
  • Scout begins to chastise him for this, however Calpurnia pulls her aside and scolds her for her words.
  • Back at school, a wild afternoon ensues, as Miss Caroline is shocked to discover a bug in a young boys hair.
  • The boy is Burris Ewell, his family is one of the poorest around, and he and his siblings usually only make a few appearances at school to avoid being hassled by the law.
  • Following school, Scout speaks with her father and complains about school.
  • She claims she would be much better off being home-schooled, however Atticus is adamant that she return as school is important.
  • Chapter 4
  • The school year goes by and Scout struggles to find any joy in school.
  • She feels as if she is ahead of the other students, and Miss Caroline doesn't do anything to help her development.
  • One afternoon following school, Scout walks past Boo Radley's house and notices a tree with a knothole.
  • Sitting in the knothole is two pieces of gum, which Scout gladly takes.
  • She tells Jem of what she has found, however he is upset that she chewed the gum.
  • Later on the two are surprised to discover two Indian-head pennies in the same knothole; it appears as if these are gifts from Boo.
  • Eventually the school year ends and Scout and Jem can enjoy another summer.
  • Sure enough, Dill returns again for the summer.
  • The three begin their usual games again, and one day while rolling in an old tire, they are brought to the doorstep of Boo Radley's, however nothing comes of it.
  • Following the close call, the group makes up a new game in which they act out what they believe it's like to live in the Radley household.
  • Atticus catches them playing this game and scolds them, however they lie and convince him otherwise.
  • Chapter 5
  • As the summer progresses Dill and Jem begin spending more time together, and Scout becomes left out.
  • She decides to start spending time with a widow neighbor of theirs, Miss Atkinson.
  • Miss Atkinson is a kind woman and enjoys the company.
  • She tells Scout what she knows of the Radley family, and explains that Boo is the product of an abusive, hardcore-Baptist father who is believed to be dead now.
  • Jem and Dill have decided to invite Boo out for ice cream by trying to put a note on his window with a fishing pole, however they are unsuccessful, and Atticus scolds them for bothering Boo.
  • Chapter 6
  • For the rest of the summer Dill and Jem stay away from the Radley's place, however on the day before Dill leaves, he and Jem decide to go against Atticus' word and sneak up to the house.
  • The boys are joined by Scout and creep around the house looking through windows.
  • The three believe they see someone inside, however they are startled by a noise, and begin to flee.
  • They hear a shotgun go off and run as fast as they can, even jumping a fence.
  • On the fence Jem loses his pants, yet they continue on and arrive back at the Finch household.
  • Outside the home, a group has gathered discussing the Radley's.
  • Mr. Radley claims that a Negro had been sneaking around his house so he chased him away with a shotgun.
  • Atticus is however suspicious of the children and their arrival, particularly the fact that Jem has no pants.
  • Dill thinks quickly and explains that they were playing strip poker; this is upsetting to Atticus, however less so than if he learned they were near the Radley house.
  • Chapter 7
  • A few days following the incident, when school has already started, Jem has a talk with Scout.
  • Jem explains that he went back to retrieve his pants after the incident, and to his surprise they were fixed and folded on the fence.
  • Throughout the next several weeks Scout and Jem find several more gifts in the knothole; a bundle of twine, two soap figurines, a pocket watch, gum, and a medal.
  • The children are thrilled, however one day they find the knothole has been filled in.
  • Jem has the courage to ask Mr. Radley (Boo's brother) about why it was filled in, he explains that it was to prevent the tree from dying.
  • During the same time period Scout is experiencing more trouble at school, this time in the second grade, however Jem encourages her to push through
  • Chapter 8
  • The winter arrives, and for the first time in several years the town is able to enjoy a real winter of sorts.
  • Snow falls, school is cancelled, and the children play outside.
  • Scout and Gem decide to make a snowman, however the process is made difficult by a lack of snow.
  • They do eventually build one, and dress it to resemble a neighbor of theirs, Mr. Avery.
  • Atticus is surprised by the sight as it is almost uncanny, he therefore has the kids disguise it.
  • One evening Scout is awakened by her father, he dresses her in a robe and takes her outside where a spectacle is brewing.
  • Their neighbor, Miss Maudie's home is on fire.
  • As a crowd gathers, the house burns to the ground despite attempts by the fire department to put it out.
  • Following the ordeal Atticus notices that Scout now has a blanket wrapped around her shoulders.
  • She is however unaware of how it got there, Jem knows though, as it was Boo Radley who provided it.
  • Jem goes on to explain to his father how Boo had been giving the children gifts.
  • Atticus is shocked by what he learns; yet he asks them to keep quiet about it.
  • The following morning Miss Maudie is surprisingly upbeat for the events from the night before.
  • She explains to the children that she plans on building a smaller house and nicer garden, seeing the bright side of a bad situation.
  • Chapter 9
  • School becomes very difficult for Scout in the upcoming weeks, as Atticus has been chosen to be the lawyer of a black man, Tom Robinson, who is on trial for rape.
  • During school one day, she nearly picks a fight with a boy who rants about how her father is defending black man.
  • In regards to the trial, Atticus claims that he is doing his moral duty by defending the man.
  • Over the next few weeks the family is joined by Atticus' brother Jack who stays with the family.
  • Jack spends a lot of time around Scout and is shocked to learn that she has begun to swear, and often too.
  • He scolds her for her ways.
  • The family travels to the old Finch farm where Atticus and Jack's sister Alexandra's family lives.
  • Scout despises the holidays with the other Finches' as she must spend time with Alexandra's grandson, as well as put up with Alexandra's constant nagging.
  • Scout begins to hate Francis (Alexandra's grandson) as he constantly makes fun of Dill, and trashes Atticus for his sympathy of blacks.
  • Furious, Scout beats up Francis, however she is punished for her actions.
  • After returning home, Scout tells Jack about why she attacked Francis.
  • Francis forgives Scout, as he cannot believe what Francis said.
  • An evening a few days later, Scout overhears her father and uncle talking about the Tom Robinson trial.
  • Atticus explains that Tom is surly innocent, however an all white jury will never acquit him.
  • Chapter 10
  • Atticus is described as being much older than the typical parents in Maycomb.
  • With his age, he also has a different attitude, preferring to read and learn, than hunt and fish.
  • Yet one day he proves that his age does not cast him out from the other adults, as he protects the family from a rabid dog by shooting it from a very long distance.
  • Following this Scout learns from a neighbor that Atticus used to be the best shot around.
  • Scout loves to brag about this, however Jem stops her as he believes that Atticus would not be proud of her bragging.
  • Chapter 11
  • One afternoon Scout and Jem walk into town using the same route they normally do.
  • This trip is much more eventful than usual as they are shocked at the words they hear from a woman whose home they pass.
  • The woman is Mrs. Dubose, an elderly woman very stuck in her southern ways.
  • Mrs. Dubose yells harsh words about Atticus, and tells the children that he is on the same level as the blacks, which she calls trash, that he works with.
  • Jem is outraged and begins destroying the bushes in her garden.
  • When Atticus learns of this he punishes him by making him visit Mrs. Dubose every day for a month and spend time reading to her.
  • Shortly after the punishment is over Mrs. Dubose dies and Atticus explains that it was due to her addiction to morphine.
  • When she died she left a gift for Jem, a box containing a flower from the garden he trashed earlier.
  • Part 2: Chapter 12
  • The school year ends again and the children are excited for the return of Dill, however he does not move to Maycomb again as he is staying with his mother for the summer.
  • Scout (now 12) and Jem begin to feud as Jem feels that she is annoying him and should make other friends.
  • The summer is made worse for Scout as Atticus is away from home often as he travels to the capital every day for work over the course of a few weeks.
  • Calpurnia cares for the kids during this time, and one Sunday decides to bring them along to her church.
  • Calpurnia's church is an all black church, but despite this, they are very welcoming to Scout and Jem.
  • While at church a collection is organized for Tom Robinson's wife who is struggling while her husband is in jail.
  • Scout discovers that Tom's accuser is Bob Ewell, a very poor and dishonorable man.
  • She is shocked that anyone could believe him.
  • Following church the children arrive home to discover that their Aunt Alexandra is at their home.
  • Chapter 13
  • Aunt Alexandra explains that she wants to spend time with the children and provide them with a feminine presence since they no longer have a mother.
  • Aunt Alexandra is quick to make herself known in town, and becomes involved in various social groups.
  • It appears to Scout and Jem that Aunt Alexandra is very proud of being a Finch, and makes it aware to everyone, however the same fervor is not shared by them.
  • She even urges Atticus to instill the idea of family and heritage into the children, however Scout is put to the verge of tears by his attempts.
  • Chapter 14
  • As the trial of Tom Robinson approaches Scout and Jem become outcasts in the community.
  • Scout, having overheard what Tom Robinson was accused of in church, asks her father to explain rape to her.
  • Atticus does not discus it, however Alexandra is quick to question where she heard it mentioned.
  • Learning that Scout heard of the accusations at church with Calpurnia, Alexandra becomes upset and demands the children not to return again.
  • Alexandra even tries to convince Atticus to let Calpurnia go, however Atticus will hear nothing of this.
  • Later on Jem lectures Scout for riling Alexandra up causing Scout to become enraged and attack him.
  • Atticus stops the fight and sends them both to their rooms.
  • Before Scout is able to settle on her bed she notices something hiding underneath.
  • Immediately she alerts Jem and they discover that Dill is hiding beneath, Dill explains that he could not stand living with his mother and new father.
  • The two get food and explain the situation to Atticus before Dill settles in for the night to stay with them.
  • Chapter 15
  • Several days later the Finches are visited by a large group of men, which is led by Heck Tate.
  • The groups arrival has to due with the upcoming trial, and it is quite clear that the possibility of a lynch mob will come for Tom.
  • Around the same time Jem has overheard Alexandra arguing with Atticus about the trial, it appears as if she is on the side of the accuser and disapproves of Atticus' defense of a black man.
  • The children decide to follow their father to work one day and find him sitting outside the jail where Tom is kept.
  • While they are watching their father, a group of people approaches the jail and begin to have words with Atticus.
  • They appear to want access to Tom, however Atticus refuses.
  • Hoping to help, Scout runs up to join her father and is joined by Jem and Dill.
  • The group is not dissuaded by the sight of Atticus' children and instead explains that if they don't leave, they will be in danger.
  • Scout is surprisingly able to diffuse the problem as she notices a man in the crowd to be Mr. Cunningham, the father of her classmate.
  • She seeks him out and begins to speak to him, her actions force Mr. Cunningham and the rest of the group to give up their attempt to lynch Tom.
  • After the group leaves it is learned that nearby the head of the town newspaper, Mr. Underwood, had his shotgun aimed on the group the whole time incase they tried to harm anyone.
  • Chapter 16
  • The trial starts the next day for Tom and to no one's surprise the courthouse is packed with nearly everyone from town, most hoping that he is sent to death.
  • Even those from outside of town arrive including a man named Mr. Dolphus Raymond, a wealthy man who lives on a large estate with his black mistress and their children.
  • When the courtroom opens for the beginning of the trial it is so crowded that Jem, Dill, and Scout must sit in the balcony with the blacks who have arrived to watch.
  • The trial is presided by an older judge, Judge Taylor, who runs a very informal courtroom.
  • Chapter 17
  • The trial begins with the questioning of Heck Tate by both sides of the trial.
  • Mr. Gilmer, the prosecutor begins questioning Tate about the night the alleged rape occurred.
  • Tate explains that on that night, November 21st, he was summoned to the Ewell household by Bob Ewell who claimed his daughter had been raped.
  • When Tate arrived, he saw Mayella, the daughter, had been beaten.
  • Mayella also explained that Tom Robinson had raped her.
  • Atticus now has his turn to question Tate, and Tate admits that now doctor was called for on this night, and that the bruises were only on the right side of Mayella's face.
  • Eventually Ewell is summoned to testify, however before he does Scout explains to the reader that the Ewell family lives in a very poor side of town behind a garbage dump.
  • The family is also large and no one is quite sure how many children there are.
  • When Ewell reaches the stand he tells his account of that night; he explains that he heard his daughter screaming from the woods, and when he arrived at the house he saw Tom Robinson raping her through a window.
  • Ewell claims that once he noticed Tom, Tom fled.
  • Atticus begins his questioning and wonders why no doctor was called; Ewell explains that it was not worth the money.
  • Atticus then has Ewell write his name, this shows the jury that Ewell is left-handed.
  • Atticus finishes by saying that a left-handed person would most likely strike the right side of someone's face.
  • Chapter 18
  • When the trial begins again it is Mayella's turn to testify on the events.
  • She tells the jury that she had seen Tom walking by her house; she needed help with a task and offered him a nickel for his services.
  • However, when Tom came inside, he proceeded to grab her and begin raping her.
  • Atticus begins his questioning and it is revealed that Mayella lives a sad life helping raise her seven siblings, putting up with an often drunk father, and having no time for any friends.
  • Atticus then begins to rip into her allegations by questioning why she had not put up a struggle and how come none of the other Ewell family members were aware of the events.
  • He then questions the bruises on her face, as Tom would have no chance of causing them being that his left hand is maim from injuring it as a child.
  • Atticus demands she give the act up as it is quite obvious she is lying, however she refuses, and begins to cry causing the court to come to recess.
  • In the meantime, Mr. Underwood notices Scout, Jem, and Dill in the balcony, however he chooses not to inform Atticus.
  • When the trial resumes, Tom is called to the stand.
  • Chapter 19
  • Tom explains the story differently; he had been walking by the house when Mayella summoned him in for help.
  • However, unlike her story, Mayella grabbed him and tried to convince him to kiss her.
  • Tom refused and tried to get away, however before he could, Bob Ewell had arrived and saw what was occurring.
  • Bob realized his daughter was attempting to have relations with a black man, and immediately threatened her; meanwhile Tom escaped.
  • During the testimony the court is interrupted by Tom's employer, Link Deas, who yells that Tom is a respectable man.
  • Deas is however kicked out of the courtroom.
  • It is then the prosecutions turn to question Tom, and the lawyer is able to get Tom to admit that he is strong enough to overpower Mayella.
  • He then questions why he was always willing to help Mayella, and Tom explains it was because he felt sorry for her.
  • This makes everyone in the courtroom on edge, as it makes no sense why a black man would feel sorry for any white women.
  • The lawyer finishes by accusing Tom of being a liar, causing Dill to become visibly upset in the balcony.
  • The children leave the courtroom, as they cannot stand the way Mr. Gilmer is treating Tom.
  • While outside the children run in to Dolphus Raymond, the man who lives on the outskirts of town with a black woman, and their children.
  • Chapter 20
  • Mr. Raymond is drinking from a paper bag, however he explains to Dill that it is only coke as opposed to alcohol.
  • He adds that he does this so people will think he is a drunkard, and therefore not question why he would live with a black woman.
  • Mr. Raymond also adds that he prefers blacks to whites.
  • Dill and Scout return to the courtroom as the trial is wrapping up.
  • Atticus finishes by demanding the jury find Tom not-guilty as there is no evidence to prove otherwise, and that it is more likely that Bob Ewell injured Mayella than Tom.
  • He explains that Ewell probably witnessed his daughter trying to seduce Tom, this caused him to beat Mayella, and as a way to cover this up he accused Tom of rape.
  • Calpurnia walks up to Atticus following his last pitch to the jury and hands him a note.
  • Chapter 21
  • The note explains that Scout and Jem have not been at home, and it is discovered that they are in the balcony.
  • Atticus demands they return home for supper, however he says they can return after, thinking that the jury will have already made its decision and that they will miss it.
  • Surprisingly when the children return to the courtroom, the jury is still deliberating, a good sign.
  • Jem is confidant Tom will get off, however when the jury returns late that evening it appears as if Tom is guilty.
  • Scout notices not one member of the Jury looks at Tom, a sign that they convicted him.
  • Sure enough, the verdict is read, and Tom is sentenced to death.
  • The courtroom empties, however not before the black population gestures a sign of respect to Atticus.
  • Chapter 22
  • Jem is devastated by the verdict and begins to lose all faith in the people of his town.
  • Meanwhile, the Finch family is being showered with gifts and food from the black community.
  • A large group has gathered outside the Finch house to discuss the trial.
  • In an attempt to distract Scout and Jem from this, their neighbor, Miss Maudie, invites them inside.
  • Jem explains his feelings about Maycomb, however Miss Maudie adds that the fact the jury deliberated for so long shows signs of progress.
  • When the children leave, they learn from another neighbor that Bob Ewell attacked Atticus in the morning, spitting on him, and swearing that he will seek revenge.
  • Chapter 23
  • Scout and Jem are worried about their father, however Atticus explains that Bob Ewell got his anger out of the way and will no longer seek revenge.
  • Following the verdict, Tom was sent seventy miles away to prison where he is ordered to await the verdict of an appeal.
  • However, if the appeal falls through, Tom will be subject to the electric chair.
  • Jem is devastated by Tom's potential fate; Atticus explains that being a black man in Alabama, he was doomed from the beginning despite circumstance, he however sees promise that it took a while for the jury to deliberate.
  • Atticus also adds that one man on the jury wanted to acquit, the man was of the Cunningham family.
  • Scout is very pleased with learning this and asks if she can invite Walter Cunningham for dinner.
  • Aunt Alexandra despises the idea, as she views the Cunningham's as trash. Her reaction causes Scout to breakdown.
  • Jem is able to calm her by taking her out of the room.
  • The two talk about the prejudice views in their society, and discuss Boo.
  • Jem believes he does not leave the house because he does not like the outside world, and perhaps he has a point.
  • Chapter 24
  • Later on that summer Aunt Alexandra has a gathering for her church group.
  • Scout is asked to help Calpurnia prepare for it.
  • While Scout sits among the women and listens to them complain about blacks the group is interrupted by Atticus.
  • Atticus calls aside Calpurnia, Scout, and Aunt Alexandra.
  • He explains to them that Tom had tried to escape from prison, however he was caught and shot a staggering seventeen times.
  • Atticus tells them that he must travel to tell the Robinson family.
  • Alexandra and Scout return to the meeting where their neighbor, Miss Maudie, continues to bad mouth blacks.
  • Chapter 25
  • One September afternoon, Scout and Jem are sitting on the porch when Scout spots a roly-poly.
  • Scout plans to kill it, however Jem tells her to leave it alone as it is no harm to her.
  • Scout is surprised by this as it shows signs of him maturing.
  • She recalls a few weeks earlier that Dill had told her Jem asked Atticus if he could accompany him to the Robinson house.
  • Following that day, everyone in Maycomb spoke of Tom briefly, however most believed he acted irrationally because he was black.
  • There is however an article written defending Tom by Mr. Underwood.
  • Mr. Underwood equates Tom death to murder, seeing as he was innocent.
  • Meanwhile, Ewell is heard of again, this time mentioning that he still has plans for vengeance
  • Chapter 26
  • Another school year begins, the children far more mature than the year before.
  • Following school each day, they walk past the Radley house, however much to Scout's displeasure, they never see Boo.
  • Scout recounts one of the lessons she learns from her teacher Miss Gates at school.
  • Miss Gates discusses the Holocaust and human rights, however Scout finds this very hypocritical because following the trial for Tom, she left the court room and exclaimed "it was about time the blacks learned a lesson".
  • Jem is furious, however more so because Scout brought up the trial.
  • Scout, in response, becomes upset as well and seeks out Atticus' comfort.
  • Chapter 27
  • During the month of October, Bob Ewell begins to become a nuisance in town.
  • He finally finds work, however it is short lived, and he blames it on Atticus.
  • He is then later spotted creeping around Judge Taylor's home, as well as following Helen Robinson around town.
  • It gets to the point where Link Deas has to threaten Ewell before he will leave Helen alone.
  • It becomes worrisome to everyone in town that he is on the verge of revenge.
  • As Halloween approaches Maycomb plans a community event, as the previous Halloween had been a bad experience for the town; houses were burglarized.
  • The event consists of a play held at the school, and Jem and Scout attend alone as Atticus is too exhausted to attend.
  • Chapter 28
  • On the way to the school Scout and Jem are spooked by another boy, Cecil Jacobs, who had been hiding.
  • They eventually arrive at the school where they mingle with many other children and attend a haunted house.
  • Following the haunted house the play is set to begin, and Jem and Scout both have roles, however Scout misses her role as she falls asleep.
  • Upon awaking Scout runs onto the stage only to make a scene; she is accused of ruining the play and is very ashamed.
  • Her and Jem wait for everyone to clear out before heading home.
  • On the walk home, the two seem to be followed by someone, however they are unsure of who it is.
  • They continue on and the pursuer only gets closer, eventually quickening his pace to a run.
  • The pursuer eventually catches up to the two and grabs on to Jem.
  • Panicked, Scout searches for Jem but cannot find him; eventually she sees that another man has picked him up and is carrying him home.
  • At home Scout learns that Jem is just slightly concussed and has a broken arm, she also learns from Heck Tate who has arrived at the home that the attacker was Bob Ewell and that Ewell has been found dead under a nearby tree.
  • Chapter 29
  • Scout begins to relate the events to Atticus, Tate, and Aunt Alexandra.
  • As she tells of the attack she notices in the corner of the room a very pale man.
  • She discovers that he is Boo Radley, and that he is responsible for saving both Scout and Jem's lives.
  • Chapter 30
  • Boo and Scout sit outside on the porch as the others discuss the night's events.
  • They work up a plan on how to tell the story, as it is quite clear that Boo killed Ewell, however that would cause drama in the town.
  • They instead decide to say that Ewell, in a drunken rage, fell on his knife.
  • It is considered justified for his acts on that night, as well as his responsibility in Tom Robinson's death.
  • Chapter 31
  • Scout proceeds to take Boo into Jem's room to introduce him to Jem.
  • She then walks him home and says goodnight, this is the last time she ever sees him.
  • When she returns home, she is joined in Jem's room by Atticus who reads them a story until they fall asleep.
